Backflow 101

What is backflow and
why does it matter?

Most people have never heard of backflow — until they get a notice saying their device needs testing. Here's everything you need to know, in plain English.

🚰
What is backflow?
Water in your pipes normally flows in one direction — from the city's water main into your home or business. Backflow is when water accidentally flows backwards, pushing water from your property back into the public water supply.

Think of it like this: imagine a garden hose sitting in a bucket of fertilizer. If pressure drops suddenly, that fertilizer could get sucked back through the hose into your drinking water — and your neighbor's too.
🛡️
What prevents it?
A backflow prevention assembly is a special valve installed on your water line. It's a one-way gate that allows water to flow in but physically prevents it from flowing backwards — no matter what.

These devices are required by Texas law at any location where contamination is possible — restaurants, hair salons, irrigation systems, car washes, medical offices, and many more.

Why do I have to test it every year?

Like any mechanical device, backflow preventers can wear out, spring a leak, or fail over time. An annual test by a certified tester ensures it's still working correctly. If it fails, repairs are made on the spot. This is required by state law — Texas state law.

How does testing work?

1
A certified tester visits your property
A state-licensed backflow tester comes to your location with calibrated gauge equipment. The visit usually takes 15–30 minutes.
2
They test the device with a differential pressure gauge
The tester connects a precision gauge to your backflow assembly and measures the pressure differential across each check valve. Results are recorded on an official test form.
3
Pass — you're done for the year
If the device passes, the tester submits the report digitally and you receive confirmation. Your status is updated in the compliance system automatically.
4
Fail — repairs are made immediately
If the device fails, the tester replaces worn seals, springs, or other parts right then. The device is retested before they leave. Most repairs take just a few minutes.

What type of device do I need?

Locations are classified by hazard level. The higher the risk of contamination, the stronger the protection required.

Severe
RPBA REQUIRED
Hospitals, dialysis centers, funeral homes, car washes, gas stations, pools
High
RPBA REQUIRED
Restaurants, hotels, golf courses, auto repair, pharmacies, vet clinics
Moderate
DCVA OR RPBA
Schools, churches, grocery stores, fire stations, office buildings
Low
PVB OR DCVA
Residential irrigation systems, standard commercial buildings

Frequently Asked Questions

I got a letter saying my device needs testing. What do I do? ›
First, check your status above by clicking "Check My Device Status." Then contact a state-certified backflow tester in your area to schedule a test. You can also contact the Cross Connection Inspector Program for a list of certified testers near you.
How much does a backflow test cost? ›
Testing costs vary by tester and device size, but typically range from $50–$150 for residential devices. Commercial devices with larger assemblies may cost more. Repairs, if needed, are usually additional. Contact a certified tester for a quote.
What happens if I don't get my device tested? ›
Under state law, failure to test can result in a compliance notice from the city. Continued non-compliance may result in water service interruption. It's much easier — and cheaper — to schedule an annual test than to deal with enforcement action.
I'm a homeowner. Do I really need a backflow device? ›
It depends. Most basic homes don't require one — but if you have an irrigation system, a swimming pool, a hot tub, or any chemical injection system connected to your water supply, you likely do. The city can tell you whether your property is in the compliance program.
How do I know if my test passed or failed? ›
Log in to the Property Owner Portal above using your email address. Your test results, pass/fail status, last test date, and next due date are all shown there. You can also ask your tester for a copy of the official test report form they submit after testing.
My device failed — is that serious? ›
A failed test means your device is not providing full protection, but it's a common and fixable issue. In most cases the tester can repair it on the spot by replacing worn seals or springs. Once repaired, it will be retested to confirm it passes before the visit is complete.
