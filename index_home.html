Backflow 101

What is backflow and
why does it matter?

Most people have never heard of backflow — until they get a notice saying their device needs testing. Here's everything you need to know, in plain English.

What is backflow?
Water in your pipes normally flows one direction — from the public water main into your home or business. Backflow is when water accidentally flows backwards, pushing contaminated water back into the public supply.

Backpressure — pressure on your side of the meter exceeds the main. Boilers, pumps, and elevated tanks can cause this.

Backsiphonage — a sudden pressure drop in the main creates a vacuum that sucks water backwards. A garden hose sitting in fertilizer gets sucked back into your drinking water when a nearby fire hydrant opens. This is the most common backflow cause in Texas.
Backsiphonage — the hidden risk
Backsiphonage happens when a water main loses pressure — from a main break, heavy demand, or firefighting nearby. The drop creates a siphon that pulls water backwards through any connected pipes.

Real Texas example: A worker pushed a garden hose into a pesticide tank. Nearby utility workers opened a flush valve, pressure dropped, and pesticide was sucked backwards into the home's water lines — and nearly into the city's main.
What prevents both types?
A backflow prevention assembly is a mechanical valve that allows water to flow in one direction only — stopping both backpressure and backsiphonage regardless of what happens to water pressure outside.

Different hazard levels require different devices — from a simple vacuum breaker on a garden hose to a full Reduced Pressure Zone (RPZ) assembly on a hospital or chemical plant.
